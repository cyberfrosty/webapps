Beekeeping

Domestic Honey bees
Beekeepers are Livestock Managers
  • Kept western honey bees are domestic livestock used to produce food for human consumption
  • Most states require or encourage that hives are registered with their agriculture departments
  • Beekeepers are responsible for the health and wellbeing of their colonies
    • Being informed of things affecting honey bees in your area like diseases and pesticides
    • Constructing, placing and managing the hives as the populations and seasons change
    • Ensuring that sufficient food and water is available over the entire year
    • Monitoring the health of the colonies over the active seasons
    • Treating issues affecting their health using informed best practices
4 P's of Colony Collapse
Parasites, Pathogens, Pesticides, and Poor nutrition
  • Parasites: These include pests like varroa mites, which weaken bees and transmit diseases.
  • Pathogens: These are diseases, such as viruses, bacteria, and fungi, that can infect and kill bees.
    • Deformed wing virus (DWV) and Acute bee paralysis virus (ABPV) have a clear varroaâ€“vector relationship
  • Pesticides: Exposure to pesticides, especially neonicotinoids, can negatively impact bee health and navigation, leading to colony decline.
    • Neonicotinoids are widely used synthetic, neurotoxic and systemic insecticides. Neonic seed coating and residential use.
  • Poor Nutrition: A lack of diverse and healthy food sources, particularly pollen and nectar, can weaken bees and make them more susceptible to other stressors.
    • Bees are active inside the hive all winter and require enough stores to survive until the nectar flow starts

Pathogens
Bacteria, Fungus and Viruses
  • American Foulbrood (AFB): A highly contagious bacterial disease that affects honeybee brood, causing a distinctive sulfurous odor and sunken, dark-colored brood cells
  • European Foulbrood (EFB): A bacterial disease affecting brood larvae, causing them to die from starvation or secondary infections
  • Chalkbrood: A fungal disease that affects honeybee brood, causing infected larvae to develop into a hard, chalky-white pupae
  • Sacbrood Virus (SBV): A virus that causes brood to die in a sac-like, deformed state.
  • Chronic Bee Paralysis Virus (CBPV): A virus that affects adult bees, causing them to tremble, shake, and eventually become paralyzed
  • Deformed Wing Virus (DWV): A virus transmitted by Varroa mites, causing deformed wings, legs, and abdomens in developing bees
  • Nosema: A microsporidian parasite that infects the intestines of adult honey bees, causing them to weaken and die
  • Small Hive Beetle (Aethina tumida): An invasive beetle that damages hive combs, feeds on honey and brood, and can transmit diseases
  • Greater Wax Moth (Galleria mellonella): A pest that can damage hive combs, particularly in weakened colonies
Varroa Mites
Varroa destructor and V. jacobsoni
  • Tiny red-brown external parasites of honey bees
  • Require a honey bee host to survive and brood to reproduce
  • Lifecycle is synchronized with that of the honey bee, making it a highly successful invasive species
  • Adult females can feed off fatbody of adults and live through the winter
  • Varroa mites are mobile and can spread easily within a hive
  • Varroa mites spread between hives through infected bees robbing and drifting between hives
  • Asian honey bees have been living with Varroa and have mechanisms to keep the population low enough
  • European honey bees do not have innate mechanisms and an infected colony may experience exponential growth of the mite population
  • First detected on European honey bees in 1963 and became an issue for USA beekeepers in the 90's

Varroa Mites
Varroa Destructor Lifecycle
  • Female mite (foundress) enters brood cell 5-6 days after bee egg was laid
  • More than one foundress may enter a single brood cell
  • The foundress and her children feed on larvae and pupae
  • The first egg is laid ~60 hours after cell is capped and it is a male
  • Subsequent eggs are laid every 30 hours or so and all are female
  • Males hatch after 5-6 days and females after 7-8 days
  • Mating occurs in the cell, and males never leave
  • On average 2.6 adult females mature from eggs laid on drone brood, and 1.6 on worker brood
  • Foundress and her adult female children leave the cell on the emerging bee
  • Female mites can have up to 3 reproductive cycles
  • Varroa mites are much more likely to lay eggs on drone brood
  • Drone cells are capped a day later than workers and emerge 3 days later
  • 500 mites (1% tested rate) becomes 1,500 mites after 15-18 days, then 4,500, 13,500, 40,500 ...
  • In late summer the Varroa population continues to grow while the bee population declines

Mite Treatments
Organics
  • Formic Acid (MAQS/FormicPro) works as a fumigant, kills mites in capped brood, safe with supers, (50° - 85°F, 10° - 29°C)
  • Thymol (Apiguard) works as a fumigant, kills mites in capped brood, remove supers (60° - 100°F 16° - 38°C)
  • Hop Compounds (HopGuard 3) works as a fumigant, kills mites in capped brood, safe with supers (50+°F, 10+°C), up to 95% efficacy
  • Essential Oil (Api Life VAR) works as a fumigant, kills mites in capped brood, remove supers (65° - 85°F, 18° - 29°C), up to 95% efficacy
  • Oxalic Acid (OAV) works as a fumigant, doesn't kill mites in capped brood, remove supers (50° - 60°F, 10° - 16°C)
  • Oxalic Acid (Varroxsan) slow release strips, doesn't kill mites in capped brood, okay with supers (above 60°F, 16°C)

Mite Treatments
Synthetics
  • Fluvalinate (Apistan/Klartan/Minadox) is a synthetic pyrethroid chemical that was used for mite control in honey bees for years. An impregnated strip is placed inside the hive and kills by contact. Wide spread resistance to fluvalinate, and residue persisting in the beeswax have rendered it ineffective.
  • Coumaphos (Checkmite+) is a nonvolatile, fat-soluble organophosphate acaricide and insecticide used to control livestock insects, mites, and ticks. Mites have developed resistance and the residual toxicity of the compound has been linked to neurological problems in bees, which may be a factor in colony collapse
  • Amitraz (Apivar) is a non-systemic acaricide and insecticide. It can be used in the heat of summer. The honey supers must be removed before use and left off until at least 14 days after the strips have been removed. The impregnated strips are left in the hive for 42 days and must be removed after no more than 56 days.

Mitigation Strategies
  • Breeding for varroa specific hygienic behaviors
    • Detection and destruction of infected pupae
    • Grooming behaviors to remove more mites
    • Entombment of infected drone brood
    • Mite infertility in worker brood
  • Breaks in brood cycle: Split colonies or cage queen temporarily to interrupt Varroa reproduction
  • Drone Comb, Screened Bottom Boards
    • Add drone comb frames, remove them regulary and freeze
    • Screened bottom boards make it more likely that fallen mites can't reenter hive
      • Not proven to work, but with white bottom board it's good for seeing results of a treatment

Western Honey bee Subspecies
Around 30 Subspecies of Apis Mellifera
  • Italian (A. m. ligustica), native to Continental Italy
  • Carniolan (A. m. carnica), native to Slovenia and Northern Balkans
  • Caucasian (A. m. caucasia), native to Georgia and Central Asia
  • Spanish (A. m. iberiensis), native to Iberian peninsula
  • European dark bee (A. m. mellifera), native to Central Asia
  • Russian honey bees are a hybrid that is genetically Caucasians with some Italian and Carniolan lineage
  • Killer bees are a hybrid of the East African lowland honey bee (A. m. scutellata) with various subspecies like Italian or Spanish honey bees
Popular Honey bees for Beekeeping
Subspecies and Hybrids
  • Italian Honey bees: Known for their high honey production and adaptability, Italian bees are a popular choice for beekeepers.
  • Carniolan Honey bees: Renowned for their calm temperament, strong colonies, and good honey production, Carniolan bees are well-suited for various climates.
  • Caucasian Honey bees: These bees are known for their strong foraging abilities, especially in cooler conditions, and their tendency to gather propolis.
  • Russian Hybrid Honey bees: A hybrid known for its resilience and winter hardiness, often bred for mite resistance.
  • Buckfast Honey bees: Bred for docility and resistance to diseases, Buckfast bees are a good choice for beekeepers looking for easy-to-handle colonies.
  • Saskatraz Hybrid Honey bees: A hybrid known for high yields, good temperament, pest resistance, and wintering ability.
  • Varroa Sensitive Hygienic (VSH) Honey bees: are specially bred for their natural resistance to Varroa mites
